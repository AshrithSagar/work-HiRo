\begin{abstract}
    Robot learning from demonstrations (LfD) is a promising approach for teaching complex skills to robots without explicit programming.
    However, generalizing these skills to novel situations remains a challenge.
    This report outlines progress in developing motion generalization techniques for dynamical systems in robotics, with a primary focus on the GPT-O (Gaussian Process Transportation with Obstacles) framework.
    GPT-O extends policy transportation methods to incorporate obstacle avoidance through space warping, enabling robust adaptation of learned motions to new environments.
    By leveraging keypoints from demonstrations and obstacles, combined with affine and Gaussian process transforms, GPT-O ensures stable, collision-free trajectory generalization.
    Preliminary demonstrations in 2D and 3D validate its efficacy for tasks like reaching and navigation in cluttered spaces.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
    Motion generalization,
    Gaussian processes,
    Policy transportation,
    Obstacle avoidance.
\end{keywords}

\section{INTRODUCTION}

Robot learning from demonstrations (LfD) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for teaching robots complex tasks without explicit programming.
In interactive LfD, non-expert users can kinesthetically guide the robot to perform tasks like pick-and-place, dressing, or cleaning.
However, a major challenge is generalizing these learned policies to novel situations, such as varying object positions, arm postures, or surface geometries.

To address generalization, task-parameterized models have been developed to adapt skills based on external parameters, such as reference frames representing objects or goals.
For instance, Task-Parameterized Gaussian Mixture Models (TP-GMM) encode demonstrations in multiple frames and combine them probabilistically for new configurations.
Building on this, Task-Parameterized Gaussian Processes (TPGP)~\cite{ramirez2024learning} leverage Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling local policies relative to each frame, with self-supervised learning of frame relevance using uncertainty quantification to improve stability and performance in multi-frame tasks, as demonstrated in simulation and robotic re-shelving experiments.

Recent advancements further enhance adaptability; for example, generalizable motion policies through keypoint parameterization~\cite{lee2024generalizable} enable robust skill transfer across varying environments by parameterizing motions with keypoints and transportation maps. Similarly, Elastic Motion Policies (EMP)~\cite{li2025elastic} provide a dynamical system for one-shot imitation learning, allowing real-time adaptation via Laplacian editing and Lyapunov functions, ensuring convergence in dynamic settings.

Interactive imitation learning algorithms, such as DAgger (Dataset Aggregation), iteratively collect expert corrections to mitigate distribution shift in behavioral cloning.
Variants like HG-DAgger (Human-Gated DAgger) incorporate human oversight to decide when interventions are needed, reducing expert burden in real-world settings.
Compliant Residual DAgger (CR-DAgger) extends this by using residual policies for compliant control, enabling safer and more adaptive learning from converging supervisors.
These methods, surveyed in~\cite{celemin2022interactive}, enhance LfD by combining demonstration with online feedback.

Dynamical systems (DS) model robot motions as stable attractors, facilitating goal convergence and adaptability to perturbations.
Motion generalization in DS involves extrapolating learned policies (e.g., trajectories from demonstrations) to novel scenarios, including varying endpoints and environmental constraints such as obstacles.
This work emphasizes obstacle-aware generalization, addressing limitations in traditional DS where static policies fail in dynamic settings.

The core contribution is GPT-O, an implementation that builds on policy transportation via Gaussian processes (GPs) and affine transforms, integrated with obstacle modulation.
Inspired by keypoint parameterization, GPT-O treats obstacle boundaries as additional keypoints to warp the motion space, preserving DS stability while avoiding collisions.

\section{RELATED WORK}

Movement primitives (MPs) provide compact, reusable representations of robotic motions, enabling efficient imitation learning, generalization, and adaptation.
Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs), introduced by Ijspeert et al.~\cite{Ijspeert2013}, model both discrete and rhythmic behaviors as autonomous dynamical systems.
A DMP consists of a canonical linear system generating a phase variable and a transformation system (second-order spring-damper) driven by a learned nonlinear forcing term approximated via locally weighted regression on basis functions.
Demonstrations are encoded into weights, allowing straightforward modulation of amplitude, goal, duration, and coupling with external forces or other primitives—making DMPs a cornerstone for trajectory generation in imitation learning.

Probabilistic Movement Primitives (ProMPs) by Paraschos et al.~\cite{10.5555/2999792.2999904} extend DMPs into a fully probabilistic framework.
Trajectories are represented as a linear combination of radial basis functions whose weights follow a multivariate Gaussian distribution learned from multiple demonstrations.
This captures movement variability, supports stochastic execution, and enables conditioning on via-points or final states through closed-form Bayesian updates.
Kernelized Movement Primitives (KMPs), proposed by Huang et al.~\cite{Huang2019}, replace fixed basis functions with kernel ridge regression, yielding a non-parametric representation.
KMPs naturally handle multiple via-point constraints, obstacle avoidance, and fusion of primitives without explicit phase alignment, offering superior flexibility for complex task adaptation in interactive imitation scenarios.

\subsection{Task-Parameterized Models}

Task-parameterized models address generalization by encoding skills relative to task parameters, such as coordinate frames.
Task-Parameterized Gaussian Mixture Models (TP-GMM), introduced by Calinon~\cite{calinon2016tutorial}, transform demonstrations into multiple reference frames and fit GMMs to each.
At execution, the models are transformed to new frame configurations and combined via Gaussian product to generate adapted trajectories.
This approach enables heuristic-free and segmentation-free learning of multi-frame skills, as shown in applications like manipulation and dressing assistance~\cite{pignat2017learning}.
However, TP-GMM relies on discrete mixtures, which may limit handling of uncertainties.

Task-Parameterized Gaussian Processes (TPGP), proposed by Ramírez Montero et al.~\cite{ramirez2024learning}, extend this framework using GPs.
Local GP policies model dynamics relative to each frame, incorporating progress variables and variance minimization for stability.
Frame relevance is learned self-supervised via another GP, optimizing likelihood on held-out demonstrations.
Evaluated on multi-frame tasks, TPGP outperforms TP-GMM in goal proximity and Fréchet distance metrics, demonstrating robust generalization in simulation and real-world robotic re-shelving.

Recent works like Lee et al.~\cite{lee2024generalizable} propose keypoint-parameterized motion policies with transportation maps for generalizable skill learning, enabling adaptation to new scenes without retraining. Elastic Motion Policies~\cite{li2025elastic} introduce a dynamical system for one-shot imitation, adapting via Laplacian editing and online Lyapunov learning for robust performance in dynamic environments.

\subsection{Interactive Imitation Learning}

Interactive imitation learning enhances standard LfD by allowing online expert feedback to correct learner errors.
DAgger (Dataset Aggregation), by Ross et al.~\cite{ross2011reduction}, iteratively trains a policy on an aggregated dataset of states visited by the learner and actions provided by the expert, addressing covariate shift in behavioral cloning.
This no-regret online learning reduction provides strong performance guarantees.

HG-DAgger (Human-Gated DAgger), introduced by Kelly et al.~\cite{kelly2019hg}, modifies DAgger for human experts by gating interventions based on estimated risk or uncertainty, reducing supervision time while maintaining safety in real-world systems like robotics.

Compliant Residual DAgger (CR-DAgger) builds on DAgger with residual policies for compliant control, enabling learning from converging supervisors~\cite{balakrishna2019imitation}.
It focuses on practical implementation in contact-rich tasks, providing guidance for effective data collection and human corrections in real-world robot learning.

These techniques are comprehensively surveyed in~\cite{celemin2022interactive}, highlighting their role in bridging demonstration and reinforcement learning for adaptive robotics.

\section{METHODOLOGY}

The GPT-O framework builds upon the policy transportation approach described in~\cite{lee2024generalizable}, extending it to incorporate obstacle avoidance through space warping. The core idea is to parameterize tasks using keypoints and deform the motion space via a transportation map that aligns source keypoints to target keypoints, while treating obstacles as additional keypoints to ensure collision-free paths.

\subsection{Keypoint Parameterization and Transportation Maps}

Tasks are parameterized by ordered sets of keypoints in the source configuration \(\bm{\mathcal{S}}\) and target configuration \(\bm{\mathcal{T}}\), typically in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) or \(\mathbb{R}^3\).
These keypoints can represent start/end points, via-points, or obstacle-related features. The transportation map \(\bm{\phi}: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d\) (where \(d\) is the space dimension) is a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism that maps \(\bm{\mathcal{S}}\) to \(\bm{\mathcal{T}}\).

The map is composed as \(\bm{\phi}(\bm{x}) = \bm{\gamma}(\bm{x}) + \bm{\psi}(\bm{\gamma}(\bm{x}))\), where:
- \(\bm{\gamma}\) is an affine transformation (rotation, scaling, translation) fitted using the Kabsch algorithm (SVD-based) on the keypoints.
- \(\bm{\psi}\) is a nonlinear residual transformation, implemented as a Gaussian Process Regressor (GPR) with a RBF kernel plus white noise for handling uncertainties.

The GPR is fitted on the residuals after affine transformation, ensuring \(\bm{\phi}(\bm{\mathcal{S}}) = \bm{\mathcal{T}}\). The Jacobian \(\bm{J}(\bm{x}) = \frac{\partial \bm{\phi}}{\partial \bm{x}}\) is computed analytically for transporting velocities: \(\bm{\dot{\hat{x}}} = \bm{J}(\bm{x}) \bm{\dot{x}}\).

\subsection{Incorporating Obstacles}

To handle obstacles, GPT-O treats them as circular (2D) or spherical (3D) entities. For each obstacle, its center is replicated in the source keypoints (tiled to match the number of boundary points), while the boundary points (sampled uniformly on the circumference/surface) serve as corresponding target keypoints.
This setup effectively ``expands'' the obstacle in the warped space, creating a deformation that pushes trajectories away from the obstacle while preserving convergence to the target.

Keypoints are constructed as:
- Source: [start point, tiled obstacle centers, source end point]
- Target: [start point, obstacle boundary points, target end point]

The transportation map is fitted on these keypoints, and the source curve (demonstration trajectory) is warped by applying \(\bm{\phi}\) to positions and \(\bm{J}\) to velocities.

\subsection{Dynamical Systems Integration}

The warped trajectories serve as generalized motions in dynamical systems \(\dot{x} = f(x)\), fitted using methods like DMPs or GPs. Stability is preserved if \(\det(\bm{J}) > 0\), ensuring the attractor dynamics remain intact in the target space.

\section{PRELIMINARY RESULTS \& DISCUSSIONS}

Simulations demonstrate GPT-O's effectiveness in generalizing motions while avoiding obstacles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{2D Single Obstacle Demo}: A cubic source curve (200 points) is transported to 100 varying endpoints around a circular obstacle (center at (2.0, 0.6), radius 0.15). Warped trajectories, visualized with plasma colormap, avoid collisions and converge smoothly. Interactive mode (Matplotlib) enables real-time obstacle dragging, updating warps instantly for dynamic testing.

    \item \textbf{2D Multiple Obstacles Demo}: Extended to three obstacles (centers at (1.5, 0.4), (2.0, 0.6), (2.8, 0.3); radius 0.15 each). Warped curves adapt to cluttered spaces, maintaining stability.

    \item \textbf{3D Single Obstacle Demo}: A helical source curve (200 points) is warped around a spherical obstacle (center at (2.0, 0.6, 0.0), radius 0.1) to a new endpoint. Visualizations (Matplotlib) show deformed space via grid lines, with color-coded displacements highlighting warp effects.

    \item \textbf{3D Interactive Space Warp Demo}: Using Viser, grid lines are warped in real-time with movable obstacles and endpoints, demonstrating space deformation for intuitive understanding.
\end{itemize}

\section{CONCLUSIONS}

GPT-O advances motion generalization in DS by integrating obstacle-aware warping with policy transportation, enabling adaptable robot behaviors.
Future work includes real-robot experiments (e.g., UR5 manipulator), multi-modal policies (position + orientation), and integration with full DS controllers for closed-loop control.
